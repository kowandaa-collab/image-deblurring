HybridBlurDM: Diffusion-Guided Deblurring — Speaker Script
============================================================


SLIDE 1 — Title
---------------
Good morning / afternoon everyone. We are Charlie and Alvin, and today we present
HybridBlurDM — a diffusion-guided hybrid model for image deblurring. Our work
integrates BlurDM diffusion priors into a NAFNet-based restoration backbone using
Feature-Wise Linear Modulation, or FiLM. Let's get started.


SLIDE 2 — Problem Statement
----------------------------
The core problem is simple: blurred images lose important visual information and
reduce image quality. This matters across many real applications — from photography
to surveillance to medical imaging.

The challenges in solving this are: recovering lost details that were destroyed by
the blur, preserving the overall structure of the image without artifacts, and
handling different levels and types of blur across diverse scenes.

Our research question is: can image deblurring performance be improved by injecting
BlurDM diffusion priors into a NAFNet-based restoration backbone via Feature-Wise
Linear Modulation — FiLM?


SLIDE 3 — Project Objectives
------------------------------
We had five objectives for this project. First, study the BlurDM model and
understand how it works. Second, analyze how diffusion priors can guide a
restoration backbone through FiLM conditioning. Third, design and implement two
model variants — NAFNetBlurDM and HybridBlurDM. Fourth, implement the full 3-stage
training curriculum. And fifth, evaluate restoration performance using PSNR, LPIPS,
and SSIM on the GoPro benchmark.


SLIDE 4 — BlurDM Architecture
-------------------------------
This slide shows the original BlurDM architecture that our work builds on.

BlurDM uses a 3-stage training pipeline shown in the diagram. In Stage 1, the
deblurring model is trained using a Sharp Encoder — it takes the sharp ground truth
image, encodes it into a latent z_S, and the deblurring model learns to use that
as a conditioning prior.

In Stage 2, the BlurDM diffusion model is trained separately using only the blurry
image — it learns to produce the same prior using the blur encoder latent z_B,
without needing the sharp reference. The backbone is frozen during this stage.

In Stage 3, everything is joined together — the Sharp Encoder is replaced by the
BlurDM prior and the entire system is fine-tuned end-to-end. At inference time,
the model runs a full reverse diffusion process on the blur latent z_B to produce
the conditioning prior — without needing the sharp image at all.


SLIDE 5 — Proposed Modification
---------------------------------
This slide shows our proposed modification to the BlurDM pipeline. The inference
pipeline works as follows: the blurred image is passed through a Blur Encoder to
produce a compact latent code z_B. That code is fed into BlurDM to produce a single
prior vector p — one compact vector representing the global blur context.

That prior vector p then drives our FiLM conditioning at each decoder scale. The
formula is: FiLM(x) = gamma times x plus beta, where gamma and beta are both
predicted from p through a single linear layer. The prior modulates the backbone
features without replacing them.

Our three motivations for this design: first, FiLM injects global blur context
without replacing backbone computation. Second, there are no iterative diffusion
steps at inference — we extract p once and run a single forward pass, which is fast.
Third, our HybridBlurDM extension adds frequency-domain processing on top of this
for better motion pattern capture.


SLIDE 6 — Dataset and Experimental Setup
-----------------------------------------
Our benchmark dataset is GoPro — the standard for motion deblurring research. It
contains 2,103 training pairs and 1,111 test pairs at full 1280x720 resolution.
The blur is real, not synthetic, captured by averaging adjacent high-speed frames
from a GoPro camera, giving diverse real motion blur sequences.

On the software side, we use Python 3.10 and PyTorch 2.x. Training uses mixed
precision — AMP — for speed, EMA weight averaging with decay 0.999 for stability,
and a combined Charbonnier plus FFT loss. All training runs on a single GPU with
CUDA acceleration.

The training configuration table on the right shows the 3-stage curriculum. Stage
1 trains the backbone for 300 epochs at 256px crop, learning rate 3e-4 to 1e-7.
Stage 1b is an extended run of another 300 epochs at a lower starting rate. Stage
2 trains the diffusion model for 3000 epochs at 256px. Stage 3 is joint fine-tuning
for 50 epochs at 384px with a very low learning rate.


SLIDE 7 — Implementation Workflow
-----------------------------------
Our implementation followed six steps. First, a literature review covering BlurDM,
NAFNet, FiLM conditioning, and DDPM/DDIM diffusion frameworks. Second, dataset
preparation — setting up the GoPro train/test split, augmentation pipeline, and
dataloaders. Third, architecture design — we built both NAFNetBlurDM-light and
HybridBlurDM-light with FiLM injection layers at each U-Net scale.

Step 4 was Stage 1 training — training the backbone with the Sharp LatentEncoder
prior for 300+ epochs with cosine learning rate 3e-4 to 1e-7. Step 5 was Stage 2
diffusion training — for MIMO-UNet and NAFNet this completed successfully, with
BlurDM trained on the blur-encoder latent space with the backbone frozen. Step 6
was Stage 3 and evaluation — joint fine-tuning at 384px and measuring PSNR, SSIM,
LPIPS, and 4-way TTA. HybridBlurDM only completed Stage 1 — we will discuss why
in the Discussion slide.


SLIDE 8 — Qualitative Results
--------------------------------
Here we show visual comparison results across multiple scenes from the GoPro test
set. For each scene, we compare: the blurred input, our model's output patch, and
the sharp ground truth patch — with a red box highlighting the region of interest.

The top row compares NAFNetBlurDM and HybridBlurDM on a street/banner scene. The
bottom left is a tree/outdoor scene, and bottom right is a flower scene.

Three key points to take away: first, both models produce sharper object boundaries
compared to the blurred input. Second, texture recovery is noticeably improved —
you can see fine detail in text characters, leaves, and flower petals that was lost
in the blur. Third, overall visual quality is better — the outputs are much closer
to the sharp ground truth than the blurred inputs.


SLIDE 9 — Quantitative Results
---------------------------------
Moving to quantitative results. The training curve on the left shows all three
models converging over 300 epochs on the GoPro validation set.

Looking at the results table: MIMO-UNetBlurDM achieves the best result at 32.38 dB
PSNR with the full BlurDM prior after Stage 3 — up from 32.23 dB at Stage 1.
NAFNetBlurDM-light reaches 31.71 dB with the DM prior after Stage 3, up from
31.29 dB at Stage 1 — a gain of +0.42 dB from diffusion conditioning.

The key highlight is HybridBlurDM-light at 31.96 dB — standalone training only, no
DM prior. Despite having no diffusion prior active, it already beats NAFNetBlurDM
with the full DM prior by +0.25 dB. This shows the architecture advantage of our
DualDomainMixer design is worth more than the diffusion prior alone.


SLIDE 10 — Discussion
-----------------------
Strengths: both models effectively remove motion blur. HybridBlurDM achieves better
feature extraction through its DualDomainMixer, which processes features in both
spatial and frequency domains simultaneously. And HybridBlurDM-light achieves
31.96 dB surpassing NAFNet — purely from architectural improvement.

On limitations and why HybridBlurDM is behind the other models in the full 3-stage
comparison: first, high computational cost — FFT operations and deformable attention
are expensive compared to NAFNet's simple gated convolutions. Second, long training
time as a result. Third, HybridBlurDM did not complete the full 3-stage pipeline.
And fourth, time was lost to model errors — architectural bugs in DualDomainMixer,
MotionStripConv, and deformable attention all required debugging and rewrites,
consuming the time needed for Stage 2 and Stage 3.


SLIDE 11 — Conclusion
-----------------------
To summarize what we accomplished. We studied the BlurDM architecture and understood
how its 3-stage pipeline works. We designed a FiLM-conditioned backbone that injects
diffusion priors at every U-Net encoder and decoder scale. We implemented two model
variants — NAFNetBlurDM-light and HybridBlurDM-light — trained with the 3-stage
curriculum on the GoPro benchmark.

The headline result: HybridBlurDM already beats NAFNet with the full DM prior by
+0.25 dB using standalone training only — the architecture itself is the stronger
contribution.

For future work: the projected full 3-stage HybridBlurDM estimate is around 32.4
to 32.5 dB based on the +0.42 dB gain observed in NAFNet. The next step is to
complete Stage 2 and Stage 3 training for HybridBlurDM.

That concludes our presentation. Our best validated result is 31.96 dB PSNR from
HybridBlurDM-light standalone, with an estimated 32.4 to 32.5 dB projected for the

Thank you.
