HybridBlurDM: Diffusion-Guided Deblurring — Speaker Script
============================================================


SLIDE 1 — Title
---------------
Good morning / afternoon everyone. We are Charlie and Alvin, and today we present
HybridBlurDM — a project where we integrate diffusion model priors into image
restoration backbones to tackle motion deblurring. We designed two lightweight model
variants called NAFNetBlurDM-light and HybridBlurDM-light, and trained them using a
3-stage curriculum on the GoPro benchmark. Let's start with some background on why
this problem matters.


SLIDE 2 — Introduction
-----------------------
Image deblurring is the task of restoring photos degraded by motion blur or camera
shake. The three main causes are: camera movement during long exposure, fast-moving
objects in the frame, and low-light conditions that force a slow shutter. This has
real-world impact — blurry images reduce accuracy in surveillance systems, medical
imaging, and autonomous driving where visual clarity is critical. Our goal was to
build a restoration network that uses a generative prior to recover the missing sharp
structure from a blurry input.


SLIDE 3 — Problem Statement
----------------------------
Deblurring is fundamentally ill-posed. One blurry image could correspond to many
different sharp originals, because the blurring process destroys information
irreversibly. We need to recover fine edges, textures, and structure from a degraded
signal — while avoiding common failure modes like ringing artifacts or over-sharpening.
Our key research question is: can we use a diffusion model's latent prior — without
running the full iterative reverse diffusion at test time — to guide a fast single-pass
restoration network?


SLIDE 4 — Project Objectives
------------------------------
We set five concrete objectives. First, deeply study the BlurDM architecture and
understand how it encodes blur into a compact latent prior. Second, understand how
FiLM conditioning injects that prior into a restoration backbone without iterative
sampling. Third, design two model variants at different complexity levels. Fourth,
implement the full 3-stage training curriculum: backbone training, then diffusion
training, then joint fine-tuning. And fifth, evaluate all models on GoPro using PSNR,
SSIM, and LPIPS — the three standard metrics for image restoration quality.


SLIDE 5 — BlurDM Overview
--------------------------
The key innovation of BlurDM versus standard DDPM is its forward process. Standard
DDPM adds only Gaussian noise at each step. BlurDM's forward process simultaneously
introduces blur AND noise — directly modeling the physical degradation in a real camera.
The reverse process then learns to undo this. Crucially for our use: we don't need to
run all T reverse steps at inference. We extract the blurry image's latent vector z_B
by passing it through the Blur Encoder, then run one pass through BlurDM to get a
prior vector p. This prior p feeds our restoration backbone via FiLM conditioning —
one forward pass, no iterative sampling overhead.


SLIDE 6 — BlurDM Architecture (3-Stage Pipeline)
-------------------------------------------------
The full pipeline runs in three stages. Stage 1 trains the restoration backbone using
a Sharp LatentEncoder — this encoder processes the sharp ground truth image to produce
a reference prior, teaching the backbone how to correctly use FiLM conditioning
signals.

Stage 2 freezes the backbone and trains the BlurDM diffusion model in the
blur-encoder latent space — teaching it to generate a prior from a blurry input
instead of requiring the sharp ground truth.

Stage 3 is the integration: we replace the Sharp Encoder with the trained BlurDM
prior and fine-tune everything end-to-end at higher 384px crop resolution — this is
where the model learns to use a real diffusion prior at inference time.


SLIDE 7 — Proposed Method (FiLM + Two Variants)
------------------------------------------------
FiLM conditioning is mathematically simple: output = gamma × feature map + beta.
Gamma and beta are predicted from the prior vector p through a single linear layer.
A key initialization: gamma=1, beta=0 at the start so FiLM begins as an identity
operation — the backbone learns clean deblurring first, and prior conditioning
activates gradually as training proceeds.

We designed two variants:
  - NAFNetBlurDM-light (~18M params): standard NAFBlocks with FiLM at each U-Net scale
  - HybridBlurDM-light (~22M params): replaces each encoder block with our custom
    DualDomainMixer, which processes features in both spatial and frequency domains
    simultaneously via FFT, plus MotionStripConv for direction-aware motion blur
    handling, a GatedFFN, and a deformable attention bottleneck.


SLIDE 8 — Dataset & Experimental Setup
---------------------------------------
We train on the GoPro dataset — 2103 training pairs and 1111 test pairs at 1280x720
resolution, with real motion blur captured by a GoPro camera at high frame rate and
averaged down. Training uses mixed precision AMP for speed, EMA weight averaging with
0.999 decay for stability, and a combined loss: Charbonnier for pixel accuracy plus an
FFT loss for frequency fidelity.

One important trick: 4-way Test-Time Augmentation. At inference we run the model on
the original image, horizontal flip, vertical flip, and both flips combined — then
average the four predictions. This gives a free +0.2 to 0.3 dB PSNR improvement at
zero training cost.


SLIDE 9 — Implementation Workflow
-----------------------------------
Our implementation followed six steps. We started with a literature review covering
BlurDM, NAFNet, FiLM conditioning, and DDPM. Then prepared the GoPro dataset pipeline.
Designed both model architectures. Trained Stage 1 backbones for 300+ epochs. Ran
Stage 2 diffusion training — for MIMO-UNet and NAFNet, this completed successfully.
Finally evaluated with PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, and TTA.

HybridBlurDM completed Stage 1 but did not progress to Stage 2 and 3 — we will
discuss why on the Discussion slide.


SLIDE 10 — Qualitative Results
--------------------------------
Here are qualitative comparisons on four scenes from the GoPro test set. Each panel
shows a red-cropped region from the full blurred image, followed by three patches:
the blurred input, our model's output, and the sharp ground truth. Row 1 is
NAFNetBlurDM-light with the full DM prior. Row 2 is HybridBlurDM-light standalone.

Both models successfully remove motion blur — sharp edges and text characters are
recovered. HybridBlurDM shows competitive or better fine texture recovery, visible
especially in Scene 1 (text/banners) and Scene 2 (flower petals and leaves).

Note: HybridBlurDM uses tile-based inference here — we process the full 1280x720
image as overlapping 128x128 tiles blended with a Hann window — because its FFT
components were trained on 128px patches and produce degraded output at full
resolution. This is addressed in the Discussion.


SLIDE 11 — Quantitative Results
---------------------------------
Moving to quantitative results. MIMO-UNetBlurDM, which completed the full 3-stage
training, achieves 32.38 dB — the best of our three models. NAFNetBlurDM-light reaches
31.71 dB with the DM prior, up from 31.29 dB at Stage 1 — a +0.42 dB gain from
diffusion conditioning. HybridBlurDM-light achieves 31.96 dB, but this is standalone
training only, no DM prior.

Two things to highlight:
  1. HybridBlurDM already beats NAFNet-with-DM by +0.25 dB using only standalone
     training — the architecture itself is the stronger one.
  2. The training curve shows all three models converging over 300 epochs — HybridBlurDM
     and NAFNet are close, while MIMO-UNet plateaus higher due to its more powerful
     backbone.


SLIDE 12 — Discussion
-----------------------
Strengths: both models effectively remove motion blur across varied scenes.
HybridBlurDM's DualDomainMixer gives it an edge — processing features in both spatial
and frequency domains lets it capture motion patterns that pure spatial convolutions
miss. The numbers confirm this: 31.96 dB beats NAFNetBlurDM WITH the DM prior at
31.71 dB — the architecture advantage alone is worth +0.25 dB.

Limitations: the model is computationally heavy — FFT operations and deformable
attention are much more expensive than NAFNet's simple gated convolutions. More
importantly, HybridBlurDM did not complete the full 3-stage pipeline. The root cause:
we spent significant time debugging architectural bugs — the DualDomainMixer FFT
integration, MotionStripConv kernel shapes, and deformable attention offsets all had
tensor shape mismatches that required multiple rewrites, consuming the time we needed
for Stage 2 and Stage 3.


SLIDE 13 — Conclusion
-----------------------
In summary, we implemented and evaluated three FiLM-conditioned deblurring models on
GoPro. The key result: HybridBlurDM at 31.96 dB beats NAFNetBlurDM with the full
diffusion prior at 31.71 dB — purely from architectural advantage. MIMO-UNetBlurDM at
32.38 dB shows what is achievable with the full 3-stage pipeline.

Projection: based on the observed +0.42 dB gain from Stage 1 to Stage 3 in NAFNet,
we estimate HybridBlurDM with full 3-stage training would reach approximately
32.4-32.5 dB — competitive with or potentially exceeding MIMO-UNetBlurDM.

Next practical steps: complete Stage 2 and Stage 3 training for HybridBlurDM, and
train with larger crop sizes so the FFT components generalize to full 1280x720
inference without tile stitching.


SLIDE 14 — Thank You
----------------------
That concludes our presentation. Our best validated result is 31.96 dB PSNR from
HybridBlurDM-light standalone training, with an estimated ~32.5 dB projected if the

Thank you.
